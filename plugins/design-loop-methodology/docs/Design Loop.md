# Solving a Design Problem Means Moving Around the Triangle

A good design process is not just analytical, intuitive, or experimental. It needs all three.

When approaching a visual or interactive design problem, it helps to move between three modes of thinking: **direct perception**, **systemic thinking**, and **embodied action**. Each mode reveals something different about the problem. Each also has a failure mode when used alone.

## 1. Direct perception: look at the thing itself

Start by noticing what is actually happening.

Before reaching for frameworks or explanations, look closely. What do you see? What feels clear, confusing, heavy, fast, awkward, elegant, or broken? What does the interface seem to be asking of you? Where does your attention go first? Where does it stall?

This is the mode of observation, intuition, taste, and presence. It is especially important in visual and interactive design because many design problems are felt before they are explained. A layout can be technically correct and still feel wrong. A flow can make logical sense and still create friction.

Direct perception helps you stay close to reality.

Its failure mode is **impressionism**. If you only rely on what something feels like, you risk mistaking your own bias, mood, or preference for truth.

## 2. Systemic thinking: make a model

Once you have observed the problem, step back and model it.

What pattern explains what you are seeing? Is this an information architecture problem, a hierarchy problem, a feedback problem, a state-management problem, a trust problem, a pacing problem, or a mismatch between user intent and system behavior?

This is the mode of frameworks, abstractions, principles, taxonomies, and maps. It helps you name the forces at work. It turns scattered observations into something you can reason about.

Systemic thinking helps you avoid treating symptoms as causes. Instead of saying “this screen feels cluttered,” you might realize the real issue is that every element has the same visual weight. Instead of saying “users are confused,” you might realize the system is failing to show state, consequence, or next action.

Its failure mode is **over-abstraction**. If you only think in models, you can become detached from the actual artifact. You may produce elegant explanations that do not improve the experience.

## 3. Embodied action: touch reality and get feedback

Design problems are not solved only by thinking. They are solved by acting.

Sketch the layout. Rewrite the label. Prototype the interaction. Move the button. Remove half the options. Put the design in front of someone. Watch what happens. Ship a small version. Try the uncomfortable thing.

This is the mode of experiment, craft, testing, conversation, feedback, practice, and iteration. It turns ideas into contact with reality.

Embodied action is where design becomes concrete. Many answers only appear once you make something. The act of building exposes constraints, tradeoffs, and opportunities that were invisible in the abstract.

Its failure mode is **thrashing**. If you only act, you may keep changing things without understanding why. You can optimize locally, chase reactions, and mistake motion for progress.

## The best design process moves between all three

Strong design thinking usually moves around the triangle:

**Notice reality → model it → act on it → observe feedback → revise the model.**

Or more simply:

**perception → abstraction → action → feedback.**

You look closely at the thing itself. You form a model of what is happening. You make a change. You observe what the change reveals. Then you update your understanding.

The key is not to pick one mode as the “right” way to design. The key is to keep circulating.

When you feel lost, return to perception: _What is actually happening?_

When the problem feels messy, move to abstraction: _What model explains this?_

When the thinking gets stuck, move into action: _What can I try that will teach me something?_

A design problem becomes clearer when it is seen, modeled, and tested. Good design judgment comes from the movement between these modes, not from any one of them alone.
