# How a founder arrives at focus

A solo founder's backlog is a graveyard of good ideas. Every one of them
felt urgent the day it was added. Looked at one at a time — should I ship
the referral feature, fix the slow dashboard, redo onboarding, chase the
enterprise lead who emailed — each looks like a yes. Decided one at a
time, they fight each other, and you end up half-doing a little of
everything: every bet under-validated, nothing actually moving a number.

The fix is to stop treating focus as a stack of separate yes/no calls and
start treating it as one finite resource you're spending against
*outcomes you can name* — revenue, retention, activation.

This doc recasts the **Priority budget** lens from
`project-loop-methodology` onto founder outcomes. That lens is about
spending a builder's attention and an agent's context across units of
*delegated work*. This one is about spending a founder's *own* finite
weeks across *bets* — and it judges each bet not by how much work it is,
but by which outcome it claims to move and whether anything backs that
claim. Different surface (your roadmap, not an agent's prompt), different
five mechanisms. The shape — one finite budget, five mechanisms with one
job each, a squint test — is borrowed on purpose; the vocabulary is not.

## Focus is a budget

You get a few real working weeks per quarter. Not the calendar weeks —
the ones left after support, sales, the outage, the tax thing. Call that
finite capacity the **focus budget**. Every bet you keep alive on the
roadmap draws from it: it occupies a slot in your head, it accretes
half-finished work, it's one more thing you "should get back to." Spend
too little distinguishing your bets and they all read as equally
important, so you drift to whatever shouted loudest this morning. Spend
the whole budget on one bet and you starve the others — including,
routinely, the one that would actually have moved the number.

Focus isn't a mood you summon. It's what's left when you've spent the
budget cleanly. The skill isn't "want the right thing more." It's
"allocate a few weeks across a few bets without overspending, and tie
each spend to an outcome you can later check."

## Five mechanisms, one job each

A founder spends focus through *how each bet is framed and carried on the
roadmap*. Five mechanisms — and the discipline is that each does exactly
one job:

- **Outcome prominence** — which named outcome the bet leads with.
  The cheapest separator. Does the bet say *"lift trial→paid activation
  on the pricing surface"* — a number on a surface — or does it just say
  "improve pricing" / "v2" / sit at the top because it's new and loud?
  Prominence is spent well when an outcome leads; wasted when urgency
  leads.
- **Evidence backing** — what makes you believe this bet moves that
  outcome. A churn cohort, ten support tickets, a funnel drop in the
  analytics wiring, a competitor teardown — versus a hunch or one loud
  customer. Evidence is finite; the question per bet is whether the
  backing is proportional to the weeks the bet will cost.
- **Scope commitment** — how many weeks, how much surface, how
  irreversible. A two-day landing-copy test and a six-week pricing-and-
  billing rebuild are different spends. Scope is the mechanism founders
  most often leave undeclared, which is how a "quick" bet eats a quarter.
- **Validation coverage** — what would prove the outcome actually moved,
  decided *before* you build. Which metric, on which surface, by how much,
  by when — versus "ship it and see," which in practice means never
  checking. Proving the *outcome* moved is a bigger spend than proving the
  feature shipped.
- **Attention cadence** — how often you revisit the bet while it's live:
  whether it's a check-the-number-weekly bet, a glance-monthly bet, or a
  set-and-forget. Re-opening every bet every week is itself a cost, and
  it's the cost founders pay without noticing.

The most common failure is stacking. One bet leads the roadmap AND is the
only one with real evidence AND is scoped to a quarter AND is the only one
with a validation target AND is the one you check daily — so the whole
budget is on a single bet, and every other one is a label with nothing
behind it. The stacked bet screams; the rest go silent. And you can't
tell whether it leads because it's the right bet or just because it's the
loud one.

One bet, one mechanism — two at most when the bet genuinely earns it.

## The mechanisms trade against each other

This is what turns five rules into a system: you can't move one knob
without it pulling the others.

**Scope commitment drains validation coverage.** The bigger a bet's
scope, the longer until you learn anything from it — and the more bets
you've committed deeply, the fewer you can actually instrument and check
before you stop checking carefully. The moment more bets are live than you
can validate, validation stops meaning anything: everything is "in
flight," nothing is *the* thing you're proving this month. A healthy
quarter commits real scope to fewer bets than you could technically run,
so the validation attention to actually read the result is the focus
signal that survives.

**Validation coverage has exactly one meaning.** If every bet gets the
same vague "we'll see how it does," validation now means "it shipped" —
i.e., nothing. When a bet *genuinely* needs proof that retention moved,
there's no information left in the word. Spend a real validation target
on the outcomes that would sink the business if they're silently flat,
not on every bet that touched a surface.

**Outcome prominence has exactly one meaning.** If every roadmap item is
"high impact" and none names a number on a surface, prominence is
decoration. So when a bet *genuinely* carries the outcome you're betting
the quarter on, it can't stand out. Spend prominence on the named,
checkable outcome — not on impact adjectives.

## Start from the work mode, not the bets

Before you allocate, name what kind of founder work mode you're in,
because it sets how much you should differentiate between bets:

- A **searching** mode (pre-PMF, you don't yet know what moves the
  number) needs *little* differentiation — most bets are small, parallel,
  cheap probes against activation and retention. Evidence-gathering and
  fast validation matter more than deep scope or daily attention.
- A **growing** mode (you've found something that works and are pushing on
  it) needs *sharp* differentiation at low slack — one or two bets carry
  clear outcome prominence and a committed scope; the rest stay flat.
- A **sustaining** mode (steady revenue, the job is to not lose it) groups
  bets by retention-defense and reliability — light, consistent attention
  cadence, deep scope reserved for the rare existential fix, most bets
  small.

Naming the mode first means you're allocating toward an outcome target
instead of chasing whatever a customer emailed about this morning.

## Consistency is enforcement, not a one-time call

Allocating the budget well on one bet is easy. The hard part is that focus
reads clearly only if the *same* discipline holds bet over bet. Solo,
there's no one to keep you honest — so if you frame each bet from whatever
mood you're in, your roadmap is sharp on the bet you added today and muddy
on the one from last month, which is worse than uniformly mediocre.

That's why the discipline belongs somewhere durable — a one-line outcome
and validation target written into each roadmap entry, a standing rule
that no bet goes "in flight" without a number and a surface — rather than
re-decided from mood each time a new idea arrives.

## The procedure

When you're actually allocating focus across what to build next, work in
this order:

1. Name the work mode and set how much differentiation it needs.
2. Spend outcome prominence first — it's the cheapest and has no side
   effects. Make a named outcome (number + surface) lead each bet until
   the top two or three read clearly.
3. If too much is crowded into "now" for prominence to differentiate,
   move the signal to scope commitment (which bet earns real weeks) or to
   evidence backing (which bet you actually have proof for).
4. Pick one mechanism per bet — deep evidence *or* deep scope, rarely both
   on the same bet before it's validated once.
5. Hold a real validation target in reserve for the outcomes that would
   sink the business if they're silently flat.
6. Hold close attention cadence in reserve for the bets whose result you
   can't otherwise trust.
7. Step back. If every bet is differentiated, remove your weakest
   separator and check whether focus reappears.

## The test

A clean roadmap survives squinting. Blur the labels — strip "high
impact," "P0," "must-do," the ordering, the date you added it — and you
should still see the structure: which bet leads, which are secondary,
which are cheap probes. If you can't, you've under-spent (nothing leads,
everything's equally urgent) or overspent (every bet competes for the
whole quarter at once). Either way the answer isn't to add another
must-do tag. It's to take a bet off the board until one clear reading is
left.
