# Intent as a Conversion Lens

A landing page has exactly one job: take a stranger who arrived with a
question already in their head and move them, without friction, to the one
action that answers it. Most landing pages fail not because they're ugly
but because they're **organized around the wrong thing** — around the
product's feature list, or the founder's pride, or whatever the page
builder dropped in by default — instead of around the visitor's intent.

This doc adapts the *intent-altitude ladder* from design-loop-methodology's
**intent lens** (see `Intent as a Design Lens.md` in that plugin) and
retargets it from app screens to **landing and conversion surfaces**. The
parent lens asks "what altitude is this screen organized at, and does that
match what the user opened it to do?" The same question is even sharper on
a landing page, because the visitor didn't *open* it on purpose — they
landed on it, mid-thought, from an ad or a link or a tweet, and they will
leave in seconds if the page isn't pitched at the altitude of the thing
they actually want. We keep the parent's core move — name the organizing
altitude, name the intended altitude, measure the gap — and we re-cast the
altitudes themselves for the conversion surface. Nothing here is copied;
the ladder below is the landing-page ladder, not the screen ladder.

## The conversion-intent ladder

A landing surface is organized at one of four altitudes. Read the page and
ask: *what is it built around? what leads, what gets the most weight, what
is the first thing a skimming stranger registers?*

### Feature altitude (lowest)

The page is organized around **what the product has**. A grid of features,
a list of capabilities, a tour of the UI, "and it also does X." This is the
landing-page equivalent of the parent's *data-model altitude* — the page
surfaces the shape of the thing built, not the shape of the visitor's want.
A feature-altitude page makes the visitor do the translation work: *okay,
but what does that get me?* Most do not bother. They leave.

Tell: the hero leads with a noun about the product ("The all-in-one X
platform"), the first scroll is a feature grid, screenshots dominate over
outcomes.

### Action altitude

The page is organized around **what the visitor can do** — "Sign up,"
"Start free," "Book a demo," buttons everywhere, a sticky CTA bar, three
different primary actions competing. This is the parent's *action
altitude*: verbs first. It isn't wrong — a landing page must have an
action — but when the *page* is organized around the action rather than
the action being the natural end of a story, the visitor is asked to commit
before they've been given a reason. Action-altitude pages convert the
already-sold and lose everyone else.

Tell: multiple competing CTAs of equal weight, a CTA above any stated
value, "Get started" with nothing yet earned.

### Outcome altitude (the target for most landing pages)

The page is organized around **what becomes true for the visitor** — the
change they get, stated in their language, before the product is even
named. "Ship your side project this weekend." "Never chase an invoice
again." This is the parent's *intent altitude*, recast: the page leads
with the visitor's goal, frames the product as the means, and lets the
single primary action fall out as the obvious next step. The feature grid
still exists — lower on the page, as proof — but it is not what the page is
*organized around*.

Tell: the hero is a promise about the visitor, not a noun about the
product; one primary action, clearly the conclusion of the promise;
features present but subordinate.

### Identity altitude (highest)

The page is organized around **who the visitor becomes / belongs to** — a
movement, a tribe, a stance. "For builders who ship." "Join 40,000
founders." This maps loosely to the parent's *state altitude* recast for
marketing: the page surfaces a belonging or a worldview rather than a
single concrete outcome. Powerful when the product *is* an identity buy
(communities, dev tools, creator products); hollow and skippable when it's
asserted over a product whose value is actually a concrete outcome the page
never states.

Tell: aspirational us/we language, social-proof counts as the hero, a
manifesto where a promise should be.

## Picking the right altitude

Higher is not better. The right altitude is **the one that matches the
intent the visitor arrived with** — exactly the parent lens's discipline.

- A visitor who arrived from a problem-aware search ("how do I stop X")
  wants **outcome altitude** — name their change.
- A visitor who arrived already knowing the product (returning, from a
  comparison page, from word of mouth) can take **action altitude** — they
  came to do the thing.
- A visitor at the top of the funnel, cold, brand-led, may need **identity
  altitude** first and outcome second.
- **Feature altitude is almost never the right organizing altitude for a
  landing page** — it's right for docs, a pricing comparison, or a
  product tour, not for the surface where strangers decide whether to care.

The gap that matters is the one between *the altitude the page is organized
at* and *the altitude the arriving visitor's intent sits at*. A page built
at feature altitude for a problem-aware visitor who needs an outcome is the
canonical conversion leak: the visitor wanted to know what changes for
them, and the page told them what the software contains.

## How this lens reads a page

1. **Name who lands here and why.** Traffic source, awareness level, the
   question already in their head. If several, name them and say which one
   the page serves by default.
2. **Name the altitude the page is organized at** — feature / action /
   outcome / identity — from what leads and what dominates.
3. **Name the altitude the visitor's intent sits at.**
4. **Measure the gap**, in one sentence: "organized at *X* altitude when
   the arriving visitor's intent sits at *Y*."
5. **Name the leak** — what the visitor has to do, or supply themselves, to
   bridge the gap the page left open. That bridging is the friction; that
   friction is the drop-off.

This lens *names* the gap. It does not rewrite the page — closing the gap
is a separate act, and a different surface (the copy, the structure, the
CTA) owns it. A confirmed match is a valid, useful finding: a page already
organized at the visitor's altitude does not need a rewrite, and saying so
plainly is the most valuable thing this lens can do for a founder who keeps
"optimizing" a page that was already right.
