# Solving a project management problem means moving around the triangle

A good PM process is not just analytical, intuitive, or operational. It
needs all three.

When approaching a real project — a delivery in trouble, a roadmap
question, a stakeholder mismatch — it helps to move between three modes of
thinking: **direct perception**, **systemic thinking**, and **embodied
action**. Each mode reveals something different. Each also has a failure
mode when used alone.

## 1. Direct perception: look at the project itself

Start by noticing what is actually happening.

Before reaching for frameworks or status reports, look closely. What's
the team actually doing? Where is energy flowing, where is it stalling?
Which conversations keep repeating? What's on the wall vs what's in the
deck? Where is the team enthusiastic, where defensive, where avoidant?

This is the mode of observation, intuition, and presence. It is
especially important in PM because many project problems are *felt*
before they are explained. A status report can be all green and the
project still be sinking. A team can hit every sprint goal and still be
building the wrong thing.

Direct perception keeps you close to reality, not the artifact of
reality.

**Failure mode — impressionism.** If you only rely on what something
feels like, you risk mistaking your own anxiety, mood, or favorite
narrative for truth.

## 2. Systemic thinking: make a model

Once you have observed, step back and model.

What pattern explains what you are seeing? Is this a scope problem? A
capacity problem? A coordination problem? A trust problem? A
prioritization problem? A handoff problem? A premature-commitment
problem?

This is the mode of frameworks, abstractions, RACI charts, and
constraint maps. It turns scattered observations into something you can
reason about.

Systemic thinking helps avoid treating symptoms as causes. Instead of
"the team is missing deadlines," you might realize that estimates were
made before dependencies were mapped. Instead of "stakeholders are
unhappy," you might realize the project's outcome was never named — only
its output.

**Failure mode — over-abstraction.** If you only think in models, you
can become detached from the actual project. You may produce elegant
explanations that do not improve delivery.

## 3. Embodied action: intervene and watch what happens

Project problems are not solved only by thinking. They are solved by
intervening.

Move the deadline. Cut the scope. Have the conversation. Reassign the
work. Surface the risk. Cancel the meeting. Add the meeting. Pair two
people. Watch what happens. Ship a smaller version. Try the
uncomfortable thing.

This is the mode of experiment, conversation, and iteration. It turns
ideas into contact with reality.

Embodied action is where PMs become useful. Many answers only appear
once you make a move. The act of intervening exposes what the project is
*actually* sensitive to — which is rarely what the plan said.

**Failure mode — thrashing.** If you only act, you may keep moving
things around without understanding why. You can chase reactions,
optimize locally, and mistake activity for progress.

## The best project process moves between all three

Strong PM judgment usually moves around the triangle:

**Notice reality → model it → intervene → observe feedback → revise the model.**

Or more simply:

**perception → abstraction → action → feedback.**

You look closely at what the team is actually doing. You form a model of
what is happening. You make a small intervention. You observe what the
intervention reveals. Then you update your understanding.

The key is not to pick one mode as the "right" way to PM. The key is to
keep circulating.

When you feel lost, return to perception: *what is actually happening on
this project?*

When the project feels messy, move to abstraction: *what model explains
this?*

When the thinking gets stuck, move into action: *what's the smallest
intervention that will teach me something?*

A project becomes clearer when it is seen, modeled, and tested. Good PM
judgment comes from the movement between these modes, not from any one
of them alone.
