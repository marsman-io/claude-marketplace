# How to arrive at priority clarity

New PMs tend to treat priority as a pile of separate decisions: should
this initiative be Q2 or Q3, does this team have capacity, what's the
exec's favorite right now. Made one at a time, those choices fight each
other, and you end up with a portfolio where everything is a P0 and
nothing actually leads.

The fix is to stop thinking in decisions and start thinking in one
resource.

## Priority is a budget

An organization can only meaningfully attend to so many things at once.
Call that finite capacity the **priority budget**. Every initiative on
the roadmap draws from the same pool — engineering capacity, exec
attention, customer communication bandwidth, marketing share-of-voice,
support team headroom. Spend too little on differentiation and the
roadmap looks flat (nothing leads, nothing motivates). Spend too much on
one initiative and you starve everything else.

Priority clarity isn't a setting you turn on. It's what's left over when
you've spent the budget cleanly. So the skill isn't "raise the
priorities," it's "allocate a limited budget across a few mechanisms
without overspending."

## Five mechanisms, one job each

You have five ways to spend the priority budget, and the discipline is
that each one does exactly one job:

- **Roadmap position** — what comes first, what comes second. The
  cheapest separator, with no side effects.
- **Resourcing** — how many people work on it; whether a dedicated team
  exists.
- **Executive sponsorship** — whose name is on it, who reviews progress.
- **OKR alignment** — whether the initiative ladders up to a named
  outcome the org committed to.
- **Communication cadence** — how often the org hears about it,
  internally and externally.

The most common failure is stacking. A new PM marks one initiative as
*P0* AND assigns the whole platform team AND gets exec sponsorship AND
makes it the central Q2 OKR AND issues a weekly all-hands update. That's
five mechanisms spent on a single initiative, while every other
initiative on the roadmap got nothing. The "priority" one screams; the
rest go silent.

One initiative, one mechanism — or two at most when context truly
requires it.

## The mechanisms trade against each other

This is the part that turns five rules into a system. You can't move one
knob without it pulling on the others.

**Capacity drains roadmap position.** Roadmap order is your first and
cheapest priority signal, but a portfolio with too many high-position
items overruns engineering capacity. The moment you commit to more than
the team can deliver, position stops being a useful signal — everything
is "next" and nothing is *next*. That's the whole reason a healthy
roadmap shows fewer initiatives per quarter than the team could ship in
that quarter. Slack is the priority signal that survives.

**Executive sponsorship has exactly one meaning.** If every initiative
has an executive sponsor, sponsorship now means "this is in the
portfolio" — i.e., nothing. When a real escalation arrives, sponsorship
has no information left to carry. Reserve it for the few that genuinely
need top-cover air support.

**OKR alignment has exactly one meaning.** If every initiative is wired
to an OKR, OKRs are now decoration. So when an initiative *genuinely*
ladders to a named outcome, the alignment can't stand out. Spend OKR
alignment on outcomes you can name, not on every project that touched
the strategy document.

## Start from the target, not the initiatives

Before you allocate, decide what kind of portfolio you're running:

- A **discovery portfolio** (early-stage product, ambiguous market) needs
  very little differentiation between bets — most initiatives are
  small, parallel, and roughly equal. Communication cadence stays low;
  roadmap position is rough.
- A **delivery portfolio** (executing on validated bets) needs a lot of
  differentiation at low slack — clear roadmap positions, defined
  resourcing, OKR-anchored initiatives. Executive sponsorship and
  cadence rise.
- An **operations portfolio** (steady state, maintaining a product line)
  groups initiatives by area — small consistent cadence, exec
  sponsorship reserved for cross-cutting bets, most initiatives flat.

Naming the portfolio type first means you're allocating toward a target
instead of decorating reactively.

## Consistency is enforcement, not a one-time decision

Allocating the budget well in one quarter is easy. The hard part is that
priority reads clearly only if the *same* allocation discipline holds
quarter over quarter. If each planning cycle, or each leader, spends the
budget differently, your portfolio is sharp in one quarter and muddy in
the next — which is worse than being uniformly mediocre.

That's why the allocation belongs in a planning framework, not in a
quarterly negotiation. Tokenize the priority levels, the resourcing
brackets, the sponsorship tiers, and the OKR ladder, so the rules are
enforced by default rather than re-litigated every quarter.

## The procedure

When you're actually allocating priority on a roadmap, work in this
order:

1. Name the portfolio type and set how much differentiation it needs.
2. Spend roadmap position first — it's the cheapest and has no side
   effects. Order the initiatives until the top three make sense.
3. If the portfolio is too crowded for roadmap-position to differentiate,
   move that signal to resourcing (which team owns it) or to OKR alignment.
4. Pick one mechanism per initiative — sponsorship *or* OKR, never both
   for the same initiative.
5. Hold executive sponsorship in reserve for the few that need air cover.
6. Hold OKR alignment in reserve for outcomes you can name.
7. Step back. If every initiative is differentiated, remove your weakest
   separator and check whether priority reappears.

## The test

A clean portfolio survives squinting. If you blur the roadmap, or strip
out the labels, you should still see the structure — what's leading,
what's secondary, what's deprioritized. If you can't, you've under-spent
(nothing leads) or overspent (everything competes). Either way, the
answer isn't to add more. It's to take something away until one clear
reading is left.
